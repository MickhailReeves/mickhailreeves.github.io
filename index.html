About me

Mickhail Reeves M.S. HFID Candidate · Bentley University

I'm a graduate student in Human Factors in Information Design at Bentley University, where I focus on the intersection of user research, information architecture, and evidence-based design. My work centers on understanding how people process information under uncertainty and translating those insights into systems that actually work.

My research projects have spanned enterprise SaaS pricing transparency, file-management workflows, the usability of AI-assisted health tools, and post-fulfillment product support experiences. I approach each project with structured qualitative methods, tight stakeholder alignment, and a commitment to findings that are actionable rather than just interesting.

My undergraduate training in psychology and computer science gives me a dual lens: I understand why users behave the way they do, and I can bridge that to the technical and design constraints teams actually work with. Before graduate school, I built experience in crisis counseling, care management, and STEM instruction, all roles that sharpened my ability to communicate complex information clearly and meet people where they are.

Closing the Post-Fulfillment Support Gap in Contact Lens Care

Project Overview

The client, a leading vision care company, needed to understand where new and returning contact lens wearers struggled most after their lenses arrived. Existing support materials existed but were not being found or used at the moments users needed them. Our team applied a three-phase Learn → Build → Test process, moving from broad exploratory research through mid-fidelity prototype development and unmoderated usability testing.

Methodology
01
Learn
Online survey (n=81) + semi-structured interviews (n=8) to map post-fulfillment pain points and help-seeking behavior.
02
Build
Thematic synthesis via affinity diagramming. Translated findings into design requirements and a mid-fidelity prototype.
03
Test
Unmoderated usability testing on UserTesting.com (n=8) across Gen Z and Millennial cohorts. Iterative design revision.
Research Scale
81
Survey respondents
8
Interview participants
8
Usability testers

Research instruments
Key Findings
1
Trusted support is not available at the moment it is needed 47% of wearers with questions went to Google; only 16% contacted their ECP, despite ECPs being the most trusted source.
2
Existing resources are not being discovered 40% of respondents believed they received no support materials. 33% did not know digital resources existed at all.
3
The core use case is normalcy validation, not information retrieval Top-requested feature: quick ECP access (40.5%). Second: a way to check if what they're experiencing is normal (35.1%).
4
Manufacturer support must earn engagement through genuine utility 81% open to manufacturer-led support, but only if discoverable, timely, relevant, credible, and actionable.

UX Research · AI Tool Evaluation
Research Study
Understanding Consumer Trust in AI-Based Eye-Health Tools
Contextual inquiry and evaluation framework for AI-assisted vision care experiences
Client
[Vision Care Client] - Confidential
Context
HF710 Applied UX Research, Bentley University
Role
UX Researcher (Lead)
Scope
Consumer-facing AI health touchpoints
Problem Statement

AI tools are becoming increasingly embedded in consumer eye care, appearing in online symptom checkers, in-office kiosks, AI-generated health reports, home vision test apps, and remote exam platforms. Despite their growing presence, many consumers feel confused about how these tools work, uncertain about result accuracy, and skeptical about whether to act on AI-generated guidance.

This study focused on identifying exactly where confusion and mistrust arise across these touchpoints, and what design and communication decisions make the difference between an experience that builds confidence and one that causes patients to disengage.

Research Focus Areas
A
Online AI eye-health checkups and symptom-checker chatbots How do users interpret AI-generated assessments? What interface patterns increase or reduce confidence in results?
B
In-office AI screening kiosks and AI-generated reports Where do clinical-context AI tools produce friction? What do users believe the AI is doing versus its actual function?
C
AI home vision test apps and remote eye exams When does uncertainty about AI reliability lead users to abandon or override recommendations?
Why This Work Matters

The expansion of AI into consumer health creates a new class of UX problem: not whether an interface is usable, but whether users can accurately calibrate their trust in what the system tells them. An AI symptom checker users dismiss as unreliable provides no value. One they over-trust can delay appropriate care. The design challenge is calibration, not just clarity.
